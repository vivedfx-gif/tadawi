Manual Therapy in Dubai

Skilled hands-on physiotherapy techniques to restore movement, reduce pain, and improve function. Our expert manual therapists provide targeted treatment for musculoskeletal conditions.

What Is Manual Therapy?

Manual therapy is a specialized area of physiotherapy that uses skilled, hands-on techniques to diagnose and treat musculoskeletal conditions. It encompasses a wide range of techniques including joint mobilization, manipulation, soft tissue mobilization, myofascial release, muscle energy techniques, and neural mobilization.

At Tadawi Clinic, our manual therapy approach is evidence-based and tailored to each patient's specific condition, preferences, and tolerance. We combine manual techniques with exercise prescription and patient education for optimal, long-lasting results.

Types of Manual Therapy Techniques

Joint Mobilization

Graded passive movements applied to joint surfaces to restore optimal joint mechanics:

  • Grade I-II: Small amplitude movements within the joint's resting range — primarily for pain reduction and synovial fluid movement
  • Grade III: Large amplitude movements reaching the end of available range — for stretching capsular restrictions
  • Grade IV: Small amplitude movements at the end of available range — for targeting specific end-range restrictions
  • Maitland Concept: Graded oscillatory mobilizations with continuous reassessment of symptoms
  • Kaltenborn Concept: Progressive traction and gliding techniques with precise force gradation

High-Velocity Low-Amplitude (HVLA) Thrust / Manipulation

A controlled, rapid movement applied to a joint at the end of its available range. This technique often produces an audible "crack" (cavitation) and is used to rapidly restore joint mobility and reduce pain. Our therapists perform manipulation only when clinically indicated and with informed consent.

Soft Tissue Mobilization

Hands-on techniques addressing muscles, fascia, and connective tissues:

  • Myofascial Release: Sustained pressure and stretching of the fascia to release restrictions and restore tissue mobility
  • Trigger Point Therapy: Sustained pressure on hyperirritable trigger points to reduce local and referred pain
  • Deep Tissue Massage: Targeted pressure to address deep muscle layers and chronic tension
  • Cross-Fiber Friction: Transverse pressure across tendon fibers to promote healing and reduce adhesions
  • Cupping Therapy: Negative pressure massage to promote blood flow and release fascial restrictions

Muscle Energy Techniques (MET)

Active engagement of the patient's muscles against a resisted force applied by the therapist. Used to:

  • Restore joint mobility by inhibiting overactive muscles
  • Lengthen shortened muscles and reduce spasm
  • Correct positional faults of joints
  • Improve muscle activation and coordination

Neural Mobilization (Neurodynamics)

Specialized techniques to improve the mobility and function of the nervous system (peripheral nerves and spinal cord):

  • Neural Sliders (Glide): Tensioning one end of the nerve while releasing the other to promote nerve movement
  • Neural Tensioners (Tension): Gentle, progressive tension on the nerve to improve its tolerance to stretch
  • Spinal Mobilization: Movements that promote central nervous system mobility

Mulligan Concept (Mobilization with Movement)

Combined active patient movement with therapist-applied joint glides to restore pain-free movement during functional activities.

Conditions Treated with Manual Therapy

  • Neck Pain and Stiffness: Whiplash, postural dysfunction, cervical radiculopathy
  • Back Pain: Mechanical low back pain, facet joint dysfunction, sacroiliac joint dysfunction
  • Sciatica and Radiculopathy: Nerve root compression and neural tension
  • Disc Problems: Herniated and bulging discs with associated nerve involvement
  • Shoulder Conditions: Frozen shoulder, rotator cuff impingement, AC joint dysfunction
  • Elbow and Wrist Pain: Tennis elbow, golfer's elbow, carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Hip Pain: Hip osteoarthritis, labral pathology, femoral acetabular impingement
  • Knee Pain: Patellofemoral dysfunction, meniscal injuries, osteoarthritis
  • Jaw Pain (TMJ): Temporomandibular joint dysfunction
  • Postural Dysfunction: Muscle imbalances and postural syndromes
  • Pre- and Post-Surgical Conditions: Restoring mobility after orthopedic surgery
  • Sports Injuries: Acute and chronic musculoskeletal sports injuries

Benefits of Manual Therapy

  • Pain Relief: Rapid reduction in pain through neurophysiological and mechanical mechanisms
  • Improved Joint Mobility: Restoring optimal movement at specific spinal and peripheral joints
  • Reduced Muscle Tension: Releasing chronic muscle tightness and spasm
  • Better Posture: Correcting postural imbalances and alignment
  • Enhanced Circulation: Promoting blood flow to damaged tissues for faster healing
  • Neural Freedom: Improving nerve mobility to reduce radicular symptoms
  • Immediate Function Improvement: Many patients experience instant improvement in movement
  • Reduced Need for Medication: Effective pain relief without reliance on painkillers

Your Manual Therapy Session

  • Assessment: Detailed examination including movement analysis, joint testing, and palpation to identify the source of your problem
  • Diagnosis: Clear explanation of your condition and what is causing your symptoms
  • Treatment: Skilled hands-on techniques tailored to your specific findings
  • Exercise Prescription: Complementary exercises to maintain treatment gains and prevent recurrence
  • Education: Understanding your condition and strategies for self-management
  • Reassessment: Ongoing evaluation of response to treatment with program adjustments as needed

What to Expect After Manual Therapy

  • Relaxation: Many patients feel deeply relaxed during and after treatment

Frequently Asked Questions

Does manual therapy manipulation hurt?

+

Most patients find manual therapy comfortable and may even find it relaxing. Mobilization techniques are gentle and should not be painful. Manipulation may produce a brief sensation, but it should not cause significant pain. Your therapist will always work within your comfort zone and seek your consent before performing any technique.

Is the cracking sound during manipulation necessary?

+

No. While the cavitation sound can occur during manipulation, it is not necessary for therapeutic benefit. Many effective mobilization techniques do not produce a crack. Your physiotherapist will select the most appropriate technique based on your condition and preference, not on producing a specific sound.

How often should I have manual therapy?

+

Frequency depends on the condition. Acute conditions may benefit from 2-3 sessions per week initially, while chronic conditions are typically treated weekly. The goal is always to reduce treatment frequency as quickly as possible and transition to self-management through exercise and education.

Can manual therapy treat the cause, not just symptoms?

+

Manual therapy directly addresses tissue-level causes of pain — joint stiffness, muscle tension, fascial restrictions, and nerve compression. When combined with exercise and education, it provides both immediate symptom relief and long-term correction of the underlying mechanical problems causing your pain.
