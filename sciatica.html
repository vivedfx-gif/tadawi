Understanding Sciatica

Sciatica refers to pain that travels along the path of the sciatic nerve — the longest nerve in your body — which runs from your lower back, through your hips and buttocks, and down each leg. Sciatica typically affects only one side of the body and can range from a mild ache to a sharp, burning sensation or excruciating pain.

While sciatica is often the result of a herniated disc or bone spur compressing part of the sciatic nerve, it can also develop due to piriformis syndrome, spinal stenosis, degenerative disc disease, or spondylolisthesis. At Tadawi Clinic, our multidisciplinary team identifies the root cause to deliver effective, targeted treatment.

Causes of Sciatica

Sciatica develops when something presses on or damages the sciatic nerve. Common causes include:

  • Lumbar Herniated Disc: A herniated disc in the lower spine can press directly on the sciatic nerve roots (L4-S3), causing radiating pain, numbness, or weakness.
  • Lumbar Spinal Stenosis: Narrowing of the spinal canal in the lower back can compress the sciatic nerve, especially during standing or walking.
  • Degenerative Disc Disease: Age-related wear and tear on spinal discs can reduce disc height and lead to nerve compression.
  • Piriformis Syndrome: Tightness or spasm of the piriformis muscle in the buttock can irritate or compress the sciatic nerve.
  • Spondylolisthesis: A vertebra slipping forward over the one below can narrow the foraminal opening and pinch the nerve.
  • Sacroiliac Joint Dysfunction: Abnormal movement at the SI joint can produce sciatica-like symptoms.
  • Disc Desiccation: Loss of water content in spinal discs reduces cushioning and can lead to nerve impingement.

Symptoms of Sciatica

Sciatica symptoms can vary widely in intensity and location. You may experience:

  • Pain that radiates from the lower back through the buttock and down the back of the leg
  • Burning or shooting pain that worsens with sitting, bending, or sudden movements
  • Numbness or tingling in the leg or foot
  • Muscle weakness in the affected leg or foot
  • Difficulty standing up or walking due to pain
  • Constant pain on one side of the buttock
  • Sharp, electric-shock-like sensations traveling down the leg
Seek Immediate Medical Attention if you experience sudden severe leg weakness, loss of bladder or bowel control, or progressive neurological symptoms — these may indicate cauda equina syndrome, a medical emergency.

Our Sciatica Treatment Approach

At Tadawi Clinic, we take a comprehensive, evidence-based approach to sciatica treatment. Our physiotherapists create individualized treatment plans based on the underlying cause, severity of symptoms, and your personal goals.

1. Comprehensive Assessment

Our initial evaluation includes a thorough history, neurological examination, range of motion testing, muscle strength assessment, and special tests to identify the specific structure causing nerve irritation. We may use provocation tests, slump tests, and straight leg raise tests to pinpoint the source.

2. Pain Management

We utilize multiple modalities to reduce acute pain and inflammation:

  • TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation): Electrical stimulation to reduce pain信号 transmission
  • Ultrasound Therapy: Deep tissue heating to reduce inflammation and promote healing
  • Cold Laser Therapy: Low-level laser to reduce pain and inflammation at the cellular level
  • Ice and Heat Therapy: Strategic application to manage acute and chronic pain phases

3. Manual Therapy

Hands-on techniques to address the mechanical causes of sciatica:

  • Spinal Mobilization: Gentle, graded movements to restore joint mobility in the lumbar spine
  • Neural Mobilization (Neurodynamics): Specific techniques to improve the sciatic nerve's ability to glide and move freely within surrounding tissues
  • Myofascial Release: Targeted pressure to release tight muscles (piriformis, hamstrings, hip flexors) that may be compressing the nerve
  • Soft Tissue Mobilization: Deep tissue massage to address muscle spasms and trigger points

4. Therapeutic Exercise

Progressive exercise prescription is the cornerstone of long-term sciatica management:

  • McKenzie Method (MDT): Directional preference exercises to centralize and reduce radiating symptoms
  • Core Stabilization: Deep abdominal and multifidus strengthening to support the lumbar spine
  • Nerve Gliding Exercises: Specific movements to improve sciatic nerve mobility and reduce adhesions
  • Flexibility Program: Stretching of piriformis, hamstrings, hip flexors, and lumbar extensors
  • Postural Training: Ergonomic education and movement pattern correction

5. Education and Self-Management

We empower patients with knowledge about their condition, including posture optimization, activity modification strategies, and home exercise programs to prevent recurrence.

Conditions We Treat Alongside Sciatica

Sciatica often coexists with or is caused by other musculoskeletal conditions. Our team addresses the full spectrum of related issues:

Lumbar Radiculopathy

Nerve root compression in the lower spine causing radiating symptoms.

Piriformis Syndrome

Deep buttock pain caused by piriformis muscle compressing the sciatic nerve.

Spinal Stenosis

Narrowing of the spinal canal that can compress neural structures.

Herniated Disc

Bulging or ruptured disc material pressing on nerve roots.

SI Joint Dysfunction

Abnormal sacroiliac joint mechanics causing low back and leg pain.

Femoral Neuropathy

Nerve damage causing anterior thigh pain and weakness.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to recover from sciatica with physiotherapy?

+

Most patients experience significant improvement within 4-8 weeks of consistent physiotherapy. However, recovery time varies depending on the cause and severity. Some patients feel relief within the first few sessions, while chronic cases may require 3-6 months of progressive rehabilitation. Early intervention typically leads to faster recovery.

Can sciatica be treated without surgery?

+

Yes, the vast majority of sciatica cases (approximately 80-90%) can be effectively treated with conservative management including physiotherapy, manual therapy, and exercise. Surgery is typically reserved for cases with progressive neurological deficits, cauda equina syndrome, or when conservative treatment fails after 6-12 weeks.

What exercises should I avoid with sciatica?

+

Avoid exercises that exacerbate your symptoms, such as heavy weightlifting, deep forward bends, high-impact activities, and prolonged sitting. Activities like sit-ups, toe touches, and heavy squats can increase disc pressure. Our physiotherapists will guide you on safe exercises tailored to your specific condition.

Is walking good for sciatica?

+

Walking is generally beneficial for sciatica as it promotes gentle movement of the spine and improves circulation without excessive loading. Start with short, flat walks and gradually increase duration. Avoid walking on uneven terrain if it worsens symptoms. Our physiotherapists can recommend the appropriate walking program for your stage of recovery.
