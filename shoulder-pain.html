Understanding Shoulder Pain

What Causes Shoulder Pain?

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body, sacrificing stability for an extraordinary range of motion. This makes it particularly vulnerable to injury and overuse conditions. Shoulder pain can originate from the rotator cuff tendons, bursa, labrum, capsule, or the glenohumeral and acromioclavicular joints.

Common causes include rotator cuff tendinopathy and tears, subacromial impingement, adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder), labral tears, shoulder instability, and degenerative arthritis. Poor posture, muscle imbalances, and repetitive overhead activities are significant contributing factors.

Conditions We Treat

Rotator Cuff Tendinopathy

Inflammation or degeneration of the rotator cuff tendons. The most common cause of shoulder pain, especially with overhead activities.

Frozen Shoulder

Adhesive capsulitis causing progressive stiffness and pain. Can take months to resolve but responds well to specialized physiotherapy.

Shoulder Impingement

Compression of rotator cuff tendons under the acromion. Causes pain with overhead reaching and sleeping on the affected side.

Labral Tears

Damage to the shoulder labrum from trauma, repetitive overhead activities, or dislocations. May cause clicking, catching, and instability.

AC Joint Injuries

Acromioclavicular joint sprains from falls or direct impact. Common in contact sports. Causes localized pain at the top of the shoulder.

Shoulder Bursitis

Inflammation of the subacromial bursa, often associated with impingement. Causes pain with movement and tenderness over the lateral shoulder.

Our Treatment Approach

Evidence-Based Shoulder Rehabilitation

Our comprehensive approach addresses both the symptoms and underlying causes of shoulder pain. We combine manual therapy, targeted exercises, and patient education to optimize outcomes.

Manual Therapy

Glenohumeral and scapulothoracic mobilization, soft tissue release targeting the rotator cuff, pectoralis, and upper trapezius. Joint manipulation when indicated to restore normal arthrokinematics.

Rotator Cuff Strengthening

Progressive strengthening of the rotator cuff muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) and scapular stabilizers. Progressive loading from isometric to functional exercises.

Scapular Dyskinesis Correction

Assessment and correction of abnormal scapular movement patterns. Retraining the serratus anterior, lower trapezius, and rhomboids to restore proper scapulohumeral rhythm during arm movements.

Dry Needling

Precise insertion of fine filament needles into myofascial trigger points in the upper trapezius, levator scapulae, and rotator cuff muscles. Releases muscle knots and reduces pain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Shoulder Pain FAQ

How long does shoulder pain take to heal?

Acute shoulder strains typically resolve in 2-4 weeks. Rotator cuff tendinopathy may require 6-12 weeks. Frozen shoulder can take 6-18 months. Chronic conditions may require ongoing management. Consistent therapy accelerates recovery.

Can physical therapy fix a torn rotator cuff?

Many partial-thickness tears respond well to physical therapy without surgery. Full-thickness tears may require surgical repair followed by rehabilitation. Your therapist will help determine the best approach based on tear severity and your goals.

What is frozen shoulder?

Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) causes progressive stiffness and pain. It progresses through freezing, frozen, and thawing stages. Physical therapy with gentle stretching and manual therapy is the primary treatment approach.
