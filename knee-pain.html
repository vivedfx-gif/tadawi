Comprehensive knee pain assessment and treatment. Our specialists help you recover from knee injuries, manage arthritis, and return to pain-free movement.

Understanding Knee Pain

The knee is the largest and one of the most complex joints in your body. It bears significant weight during daily activities and is vulnerable to a wide range of injuries and conditions. Knee pain can originate from the joint itself, surrounding muscles, ligaments, tendons, or referred from the hip or lower back.

At Tadawi Clinic, our physiotherapists perform thorough biomechanical assessments to identify the root cause of your knee pain and develop targeted treatment programs that address both the symptoms and underlying mechanisms.

Common Causes of Knee Pain

  • Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee): Pain around or behind the kneecap caused by improper tracking of the patella in the femoral groove. Common in runners, cyclists, and sedentary individuals.
  • Meniscal Tears: Damage to the menisci (C-shaped cartilage pads) from twisting injuries or degenerative changes. Symptoms include locking, clicking, and joint line tenderness.
  • ACL/MCL/LCL Injuries: Ligament sprains or tears from sports injuries, falls, or sudden directional changes. May cause instability and swelling.
  • Patellar Tendinopathy: Inflammation or degeneration of the patellar tendon, common in jumping athletes ("jumper's knee").
  • Osteoarthritis: Degenerative wear of knee cartilage causing pain, stiffness, and reduced range of motion, especially in older adults.
  • Iliotibial Band Syndrome: Lateral knee pain caused by friction of the IT band over the lateral femoral epicondyle. Common in runners and cyclists.
  • Bursitis: Inflammation of the fluid-filled sacs (bursae) around the knee, causing localized swelling and tenderness.
  • Osgood-Schlatter Disease: Growth plate irritation at the tibial tuberosity in adolescents, causing anterior knee pain.
  • Hamstring Tendinopathy: Pain at the back of the knee or upper calf due to hamstring tendon irritation.

Symptoms to Watch For

  • Pain during or after activity, especially stairs, squatting, or running
  • Swelling or puffiness around the knee joint
  • Stiffness, especially in the morning or after sitting
  • Clicking, popping, or catching sensations
  • Feeling of instability or "giving way"
  • Limited range of motion (difficulty fully straightening or bending)
  • Weakness in the thigh muscles
  • Redness or warmth around the joint
Seek Urgent Care if you experience severe pain after trauma, inability to bear weight, significant swelling within hours of injury, visible deformity, or fever with knee swelling — these may indicate fracture, ligament rupture, or infection.

Our Knee Pain Treatment Program

1. Comprehensive Assessment

Our evaluation includes detailed history-taking, gait analysis, biomechanical assessment, joint range of motion testing, muscle strength testing (especially quadriceps, hamstrings, and hip abductors), special tests (Lachman, McMurray, Anterior/Posterior Drawer, Apprehension tests), and functional movement screening.

2. Pain Management

  • Cryotherapy: Ice application to reduce acute inflammation and pain
  • TENS/IFC: Electrical stimulation for pain modulation
  • Therapeutic Ultrasound: Deep heating for tendon and ligament healing
  • Patellar Taping: McConnell taping technique to improve patellar tracking
  • Knee Bracing: Functional bracing for instability or offloading for arthritis

3. Manual Therapy

  • Joint Mobilization: Graded mobilizations to restore knee joint mechanics
  • Soft Tissue Release: Massage and trigger point therapy for quadriceps, hamstrings, calves, and IT band
  • Instrument-Assisted Soft Tissue Mobilization (IASTM): Tools to address fascial restrictions and scar tissue
  • Neural Mobilization: If sciatic or femoral nerve irritation is contributing to symptoms

4. Therapeutic Exercise

  • Quadriceps Strengthening: Progressive resistance exercises including squats, leg presses, and isometric holds
  • Hamstring Strengthening: Nordic curls, deadlifts, and hamstring curls
  • Hip Strengthening: Gluteus medius and minimus exercises to control knee valgus
  • Balance and Proprioception: Single-leg stance, wobble board, and perturbation training
  • Flexibility Program: Stretching of IT band, quadriceps, hamstrings, and calves
  • Gait Retraining: Correcting walking and running mechanics
  • Plyometric Training: Progressive jumping and landing drills for return to sport

5. Functional Rehabilitation

  • Sport-Specific Drills: Cutting, pivoting, and agility training for athletes
  • Return-to-Sport Testing: Objective criteria-based clearance for safe return to activity
  • Load Management: Graded exposure to running, jumping, and impact activities

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I rest or exercise with knee pain?

+

Complete rest is rarely the best approach for most knee conditions. While you should avoid aggravating activities, gentle movement and appropriate exercises promote healing and prevent deconditioning. Our physiotherapists will guide you on the right balance of rest and activity for your specific condition.

How long does knee pain take to heal?

+

Healing time depends on the cause and severity. Simple muscle strains may resolve in 2-4 weeks, while ligament injuries can take 3-6 months. Chronic conditions like osteoarthritis require ongoing management. With proper physiotherapy, most patients see significant improvement within 6-12 weeks.

Can physiotherapy help avoid knee surgery?

+

Yes, research shows that structured physiotherapy can be as effective as surgery for many knee conditions, including meniscal tears and mild-to-moderate osteoarthritis. Our physiotherapists will help you understand whether conservative management is appropriate for your condition.

Is running bad for my knees?

+

When done correctly, running is not inherently harmful to healthy knees and may even strengthen cartilage. However, factors like poor biomechanics, insufficient strength, sudden training increases, and improper footwear can contribute to knee problems. Our physiotherapists can analyze your running form and help you run safely.
