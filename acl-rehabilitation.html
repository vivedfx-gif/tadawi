Understanding ACL Injury

What Is an ACL Injury?

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) is one of the four major ligaments in the knee, providing critical rotational stability. ACL injuries are among the most common sports injuries, affecting approximately 200,000 people annually. Most injuries occur through non-contact mechanisms: sudden deceleration, pivoting, or landing from a jump with poor knee alignment.

The injury often involves a popping sensation, immediate swelling, and the feeling that the knee has "given way." Without proper rehabilitation, the knee may develop chronic instability leading to meniscal tears and early osteoarthritis. ACL reconstruction surgery followed by comprehensive rehabilitation is the gold standard treatment.

ACL Rehabilitation Phases

Phase 1: Protection and Early Motion (Week 0-2)

Controlling swelling, restoring quadriceps activation, achieving full knee extension, and protecting the graft. Weight-bearing as tolerated with crutches.

Phase 2: Strengthening (Week 2-12)

Progressive strengthening including squats, leg press, hamstring curls, and single-leg work. Walking without crutches achieved by 4-6 weeks.

Phase 3: Neuromuscular Training (Month 3-6)

Balance, agility, and proprioceptive training. Sport-specific movement patterns including lateral cutting, deceleration, and progressive running.

Phase 4: Return to Sport (Month 6-9)

Full sport-specific training and progressive return to competition. Must pass objective return-to-sport testing before clearance.

Our Treatment Approach

Evidence-Based ACL Rehabilitation Protocol

Our ACL rehabilitation follows the latest evidence-based protocols with milestone-based progression. We don't advance phases based on time alone: each milestone must be objectively achieved.

Quadriceps Activation and Strengthening

Restoring quadriceps strength is the single most important factor in ACL recovery. We use neuromuscular electrical stimulation, biofeedback, and progressive resistance training to address arthrogenic muscle inhibition.

Objective Milestone Testing

Isokinetic strength testing, single-leg hop tests (single hop, triple hop, crossover hop, 6-meter timed hop), and limb symmetry index greater than 90% as return-to-sport criteria.

Neuromuscular and Proprioceptive Training

Balance and proprioception training to restore dynamic knee stability. Single-leg balance progressions, perturbation training, reactive agility drills, and landing mechanics retraining.

Gait Retraining and Movement Quality

Correcting compensatory movement patterns including knee valgus and asymmetric weight bearing. Video analysis and real-time biofeedback ensure optimal movement quality.

Frequently Asked Questions

ACL Rehabilitation FAQ

How long does ACL rehabilitation take?

ACL rehabilitation typically takes 6-9 months following surgery. The timeline depends on graft type, surgical technique, individual healing, and achievement of milestones. Some athletes may require up to 12 months for full return to high-demand sports.

When can I walk after ACL surgery?

Most patients begin walking with crutches within the first week. Weight-bearing as tolerated is initiated immediately. Full normal walking is usually achieved by 4-6 weeks.

Will I return to sports after ACL reconstruction?

Yes, with proper rehabilitation approximately 90% of patients return to their pre-injury level. Return should only occur after meeting specific criteria including strength testing, hop tests, and movement quality assessment.

What exercises are important after ACL surgery?

Key exercises progress through phases: early range of motion and quadriceps activation, strengthening (squats, leg press, hamstring curls), neuromuscular training (balance, agility, plyometrics), and sport-specific drills.
